Strategic Summary

The homepage should introduce Artly as a specialized physical AI company that turns real-world human expertise, deployment data, proprietary hardware, and learning models into commercially deployable robots. The first screen needs to explain the category quickly, establish credibility, and give different visitor types a clear route forward.

The story should move from category leadership to practical deployment:

  1. Artly powers intelligent robots through commercialized robot training infrastructure.
  2. The platform makes robot training more accessible and practical for nontechnical operators.
  3. Barista Bot is the concrete proof point: a specialized robot already designed for challenging commercial environments.
  4. Deployments improve the platform over time, creating a compounding data and intelligence advantage.

Design Principles

PrinciplePlanning Guidance
Lead with category clarityUse plain language around physical AI and robot training so first-time visitors understand Artly within seconds.
Make the product realPair platform claims with demonstration media, Barista Bot details, real logos, resource tiles, and press proof.
Serve multiple visitor pathsOperators need demos and capability details. Investors need scale and intelligence-layer strategy. Press and consumers need simple orientation and resources.
Keep AI claims groundedEmphasize training, expert input, real-world feedback loops, proprietary hardware, and commercial deployments instead of vague AI language.
Show compounding advantageRepeat the idea that each deployment strengthens the system, especially near the closing catch-all section.
Use CTAs by intentReserve high-commitment CTAs for demos, expert conversations, investment interest, event signup, or lead capture. Use "Learn More" for early-stage visitors.

Risk / Quality Notes

Highest-risk items before production are numeric claims, investment language, logo permissions, and any broad AI/robotics claims that lack direct proof nearby.

RiskWhy It MattersMitigation
Overstated AI or robotics claimsFirst-time visitors, press, and investors will scrutinize credibility.Tie claims to training process, deployment feedback, hardware, and proof assets.
Numeric claims without substantiationClaims like "30-Minute Training" and "1.2M+ Actions" may require evidence or context.Confirm source, measurement definition, and approval before publishing.
Investment language complianceInvestment-facing copy may create legal or regulatory exposure.Route final investment copy through the appropriate approval path.
Logo permission gapsPublic use of logos can require partner/customer approval.Use only approved logos and maintain source records.
Vague physical AI positioningThe category may be unfamiliar to early-stage visitors.Define the platform in concrete operational language before using abstract terms.
Video accessibilityDemonstration video is central to comprehension.Include captions/transcript, strong poster image, and accessible controls.
